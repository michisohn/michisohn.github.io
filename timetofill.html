How long does a Swiss AI job stay listed?

A 5-week survival analysis of 511 listings from swissaijob.ch, snapshot 2026-05-24.

In the Swiss AI job market between mid-April and mid-May 2026, corporate listings disappeared a median of 7.0 days after posting, about 2.8× faster than startup listings (19.8 days). Five weeks of data isn't enough to make this a forecast, but it's enough to build the first honest survival model of the market and identify what drives the gap.

What this measures: Models listing lifetime (removal), not hiring outcome. Valid ~0–37 days.

Approach & framing

Why survival modeling, not regression. 350 of 511 listings were still open at snapshot time. Standard regression would have either thrown those rows away or pretended their lifespan was exactly what we'd observed so far. Both wrong. Survival models handle right-censoring natively: each row contributes "duration so far" plus a flag for whether the event (closure) has happened yet.

LLM embeddings as feature inputs. Job-description text was embedded with OpenAI text-embedding-3-small (1536-dim), compressed to 5 principal components, and fed alongside structured features (sector, company type, location, salary, employment type). The embedding components ranked 2/4/5 in global SHAP importance. The LLM signal carries meaningful weight beyond the structured columns.

The question I cared about. Which kinds of Swiss AI roles fill quickly versus linger? Not "can I shave 0.02 off a C-index." The model exists to expose patterns in the data, not to be a leaderboard submission.

The cohort

511 AI/ML listings scraped from ~100 Swiss employers. 161 closed during our observation window (event = job removed from source); 350 were still open at snapshot. Observation horizon: 37 days. About 32% of rows have a source-provided posted_at earlier than our first scrape. Those durations are capped at the observation window for the survival labels, with pre-observation age preserved as a feature.

Limitations

  • Event = listing removed, not confirmed hire. A listing can vanish because it was filled, cancelled, reposted, or pulled for budget.
  • Short horizon. Earliest scrape is 2026-04-17; the model is only valid for the 0–37-day window. Anything beyond is extrapolation.
  • Left-truncation. 32% of rows have posted_at earlier than our first observation. Durations are observation-window-bounded; pre-observation age is a feature.
  • Salary is an LLM estimate, not source-provided. Used as a noisy input, never as ground truth.
  • PCA on embeddings was fit on the full cohort, not refit per fold. Embeddings are unsupervised so leakage is minimal but non-zero.

Corporate listings churn ~2.8× faster than startup ones

The headline finding. Among the 161 closed listings, median observed lifespan splits sharply by employer type: corporate roles at 7.0 days (n=65 events), university roles at 12.3 days, consulting at 13.7 days, and startups at 19.8 days (n=74 events).

A plausible mechanism, not measured: corporate ATS systems auto-close listings on fill, while startups often extend listings indefinitely (or never close them at all). The data can't distinguish "filled fast" from "removed for policy reasons" (see the disclaimer).

In Academia, most listings stay open past our 5-week window

Only 11/67 academic listings closed during observation, vs 150/444 for industry. The KM curve for Academia barely descends. Most academic positions are still alive when the window ends, and PhD postings in particular are evergreen.

Three models, ranked by ranking quality

Harrell's C-index from 5-fold cross-validation, stratified on the event indicator: Cox PH at 0.684 ± 0.060, Weibull AFT at 0.686 ± 0.058, XGBoost AFT at 0.688 ± 0.036. The Kaplan–Meier estimator has no covariates and is a reference baseline at C ≡ 0.5.

XGBoost AFT narrowly wins (0.688 ± 0.036), but at 161 events the three models are essentially tied within one standard deviation. The honest read: covariates lift C-index ~0.18 above no-covariate baseline; the choice between Cox / Weibull / XGBoost on this sample size is a judgment call, not a measurement.

Why I kept Cox in the comparison. It handles left-truncation correctly via delayed entry. XGBoost AFT cannot; Weibull AFT cannot. With 32% of our rows posted before our first scrape, the duration cap and the entry_age_days feature are how the two AFT models reach the same neighborhood as Cox. Discarding Cox would have hidden a real source of bias. The agreement across all three is itself a robustness signal.

What predicts how long a listing stays open

Global SHAP importance on the XGBoost model. company_corporate dominates (mean |SHAP| = 0.23 on the log-time scale, meaning corporate roles get pushed toward shorter predicted lifespan). Three embedding principal components rank 2/4/5 globally: job-description semantics matter, even after controlling for the structured features. loc_Zurich rounds out the top, signalling that Zurich roles have a distinct lifespan profile vs the rest of Switzerland.

Reading one listing's prediction

A specific example: a Senior NLP Data Scientist role at ETH Zürich (Academia/university, Zurich). Still active after 5.8 days at snapshot time. The model predicts this listing will linger; the waterfall shows the per-feature contributions to that judgment.

Every contribution pushes the prediction toward "longer." Top driver is log_tag_count: many AI/ML keywords in the title and description, a marker of broader research-y roles. Then embedding PCs pick up the academic-text signal, and company_corporate = 0 (i.e. not corporate) flips the model's strongest churn signal into a longevity signal. loc_Zurich = 1 adds a smaller positive nudge.

The model ranks well; it doesn't predict exact day counts

Calibration scatter for the Weibull AFT model, events only. The model predicts longer than actual for most rows (mass above the y=x line), consistent with heavy right-censoring biasing the parametric fit. A 5-week observation window can't anchor month-and-year-scale predictions reliably. A C-index of 0.688 says the model orders listings well, not that it forecasts individual day counts.

Why Weibull and not XGBoost here? XGBoost AFT's predictions on this cohort hit the numerical ceiling for many rows (a known pathology of tree-based AFT under heavy right-censoring: the model can decrease loss indefinitely by predicting larger survival times for censored rows). Weibull's parametric form is naturally bounded and produces calibrated point estimates; XGBoost's strength is ranking, which is exactly what we use it for in the C-index comparison.

What I'd do with more data

As the event count crosses ~250, the same code re-runs with three tweaks: (1) a posted_at >= launch sensitivity slice to verify the duration cap isn't hiding a real effect; (2) refitting with scraper_name added, to test for source-specific leakage in the deactivation signal; (3) extending the observation window past 5 weeks so survival predictions beyond the current ceiling become anchored, not extrapolated. The C-index trajectory over time (does it climb, flatten, or drop as N grows?) is the metric I'd track to decide when the model is worth shipping into a product surface.
